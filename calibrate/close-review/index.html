Judge each close, tell us why.

Below are four closing moments from AI-facilitated conversations. For each one, mark whether it is a good close and give one line on why, in your own words. Trust your practice. Disagreement with the stated criterion is welcome (there is a box for that at the bottom).

These closing turns are invented for the demo. To label a real calibration pack, message your OFL coordinator.

How this becomes calibration

Each labelled turn becomes a few-shot example in an LLM judge's prompt. A judge with zero labels tends to over-approve fluent-but-generic facilitation, because a general-purpose model's default taste rewards fluency. A judge trained on labels from practicing facilitators scores like the labellers do. That is what the Open Facilitation Library is calibrating: the judgment behind the number, not just the number. The judge is advisory only. It flags for review; it never certifies or blocks anything. Your name is not attached to your labels unless you want credit.

Criterion · closing_checkpoint_discipline

Reflect back their words, then check.

A good close does two things: (1) it reflects back the participant's own specific points, not a generic "great conversation!", and (2) it asks a quick check before finishing, such as "did I capture that right?" or "anything to add before I pass this on?". Pass only if it does BOTH. Fail if it closes without reflecting anything specific, if it checks without actually summarizing, if it summarizes faithfully but never checks, or if it asks a generic "would you like to continue or shall I summarize?" after the participant already said they were done.
